Q: What is Linguistic Anthropology?
A: Linguistic Anthropology is the study of the relationship between language and culture.

Q: What are the main areas of study in Linguistic Anthropology?
A: The main areas of study in Linguistic Anthropology are language and culture, language and society, and language and power.

Q: What is the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis?
A: The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis is the idea that language shapes the way we think and perceive the world around us.

Q: What is the difference between language and dialect?
A: Language and dialect are both forms of communication, but language is typically associated with a standardized system of grammar and vocabulary, while dialect is a variation of a language spoken in a specific region or community.

Q: What is code-switching?
A: Code-switching is the practice of alternating between two or more languages or dialects in a single conversation or interaction.

Q: What is linguistic relativity?
A: Linguistic relativity is the idea that language influences the way we think and perceive the world around us.

Q: What is linguistic determinism?
A: Linguistic determinism is the idea that language determines the way we think and perceive the world around us.

Q: What is linguistic anthropology's approach to language?
A: Linguistic anthropology approaches language as a cultural and social phenomenon that is shaped by and shapes the societies and cultures in which it is used.

Q: What is the difference between descriptive and prescriptive grammar?
A: Descriptive grammar describes how language is actually used by speakers, while prescriptive grammar prescribes how language should be used according to certain rules and standards.
